02. Definition

What is Cryptography?

Cryptography is the practice and study of techniques for secure communication in the presence of adversaries. It relies heavily on mathematical theory and computer science.

  • Confidentiality: Keeping data secret from unauthorized viewers (like sealing a letter in an envelope).
  • Integrity: Ensuring data hasn't been altered. (If a hacker changes a $10 transfer to $1000, the system flags it).
  • Authentication: Verifying the exact identity of the sender or receiver.
  • Non-repudiation: Ensuring the sender cannot legally or technically deny sending the message later.

04. The Shared Secret

Symmetric Cryptography

The sender and receiver use the exact same key to both lock and unlock the data. Because the math is relatively straightforward, it is incredibly fast and used heavily for encrypting bulk data.

The Major Weakness:

How does Alice securely get the secret key to Bob over the open internet without a hacker stealing it in transit? This dilemma led to the invention of Asymmetric Cryptography.

05. The Pair

Asymmetric Cryptography

Also known as Public-Key Cryptography. It solves the key distribution problem using complex mathematical one-way functions. You generate a linked pair of keys: a Public Key (shared freely) and a Private Key (kept absolutely secret).

Public Key

Given to the world. Used by anyone to encrypt a message meant specifically for you. Once encrypted, even they cannot decrypt it.

06. Data Integrity

Cryptographic Hash Functions

Unlike encryption, hashing is a one-way mathematical algorithm. It maps data of any size (from a single word to a 4K movie) into a fixed-size string of characters. You cannot reverse-engineer a hash back into its original text.

07. Authentication

Digital Signatures

Combines Hashing and Asymmetric Cryptography. It acts as the ultimate digital "wax seal", proving the authenticity of a document and guaranteeing Non-repudiation.

1. Sign (Creating the Seal)

The sender hashes the document to get a digital fingerprint, then encrypts that fingerprint using their own Private Key.

2. Verify (Checking the Seal)

The receiver decrypts the signature using the sender's Public Key to reveal the original hash, and compares it to their own hash of the document.

08. Real World

Modern Applications

HTTPS / TLS

Secures web browsing. Your browser uses Asymmetric crypto to securely trade a Symmetric key with the server, encrypting your credit card instantly.

E2E Messaging

WhatsApp, Signal. The Private Key never leaves your phone. Only the receiver can read messages. Not even the servers can see the content.

Cryptocurrencies

Blockchains don't hide data; they secure it. They rely entirely on Hashing for immutability and Digital Signatures to prove ownership of funds.

09. The Future

Quantum Threat & Post-Quantum

Current encryption relies on math problems that would take classical computers millions of years to solve. However, Shor's algorithm running on a future Quantum Computer could use quantum states (qubits) to solve these instantly, breaking RSA entirely.

Post-Quantum Cryptography (PQC)

The industry isn't waiting to be hacked. NIST is standardizing new cryptographic algorithms (like Lattice-based cryptography) that involve math so complex and multi-dimensional, even quantum computers cannot easily solve them.

The Silent Guardian

Cryptography is the invisible mathematical layer that makes the modern digital world possible. Without it, there is no online banking, no private messaging, and no e-commerce. As technology evolves into the quantum era, so too will the art of secret writing.
